Step 1: Preparation is Everything

1. Back Up Your Data

Warning: Installing an OS can wipe your drive. Do not skip this.

Move all your photos, documents, and important files to an external hard drive or a cloud provider. Your PC will soon be a blank slate.

2. Disable BitLocker / Fast Startup (If keeping Windows)

If you plan to dual-boot, boot into Windows one last time. Go to Power Settings and disable "Fast Startup" (it locks the hard drive). If you use BitLocker, suspend it and save the recovery key.

3. Create the Bootable USB

  • Get a USB Drive (At least 8GB). Everything on it will be deleted.
  • Download the .iso file of the Linux distro you chose.
  • Download Rufus (if on Windows) or BalenaEtcher.
  • Select your USB, select the .iso, and hit Flash.

Step 2: The Installation

Booting into Linux

  1. Plug the flashed USB into your PC.
  2. Restart the PC and immediately spam the Boot Menu key (Usually F12, F8, F2, or DEL depending on your motherboard).
  3. Select your USB drive from the list.
  4. Crucial: Choose "Try Linux" first! This loads a "Live Environment" into your RAM. It doesn't touch your hard drive yet.

The Pre-Flight Check

While in the Live Environment, check these three things before clicking Install:

  • Does your Wi-Fi connect?
  • Does your audio work? (Play a YouTube video)
  • Is your screen resolution correct?

If yes, double-click the "Install" icon on the desktop and follow the easy setup wizard.

Advanced: Dual Booting (Windows + Linux)

Want to keep Windows for that one specific game or Adobe app, but use Linux for everything else? You need to Dual Boot.

How it works

You shrink your Windows partition to make empty space on your hard drive. Linux installs itself into that empty space. When you turn on your PC, a menu (called GRUB) appears, asking which OS you want to load.

Step-by-Step Guide

  1. In Windows: Open "Disk Management".
  2. Right-click your C: Drive and select "Shrink Volume".
  3. Shrink it by at least 60,000 MB (60 GB). Leave the new space as "Unallocated".
  4. In BIOS/UEFI: Disable "Secure Boot". (While modern Linux supports Secure Boot, turning it off during install prevents headaches with 3rd-party drivers like NVIDIA).
  5. Boot from your Linux USB.
  6. During the installer, choose the option: "Install alongside Windows". The installer will automatically find the Unallocated space and do the math for you.

Demystifying the Terminal

You don't *have* to use the terminal in modern Linux, but it's often the fastest way to get things done. Don't be afraid of the black screen.

Installing Software via Terminal

Instead of going to a website, you just ask the system to fetch the app from the verified repository. Example for Ubuntu/Mint (using the `apt` package manager):

sudo apt update
sudo apt install vlc

Done. VLC Media Player is installed safely.

The Linux Glossary

Linux users use weird words. Here is the translation:

  • Distro (Distribution): A flavor of Linux. The Linux kernel + a desktop interface + pre-installed apps. (e.g., Ubuntu, Fedora, Mint).
  • Kernel: The engine of the OS. It talks directly to your CPU, RAM, and hardware.
  • DE (Desktop Environment): The visual GUI. The taskbar, the start menu, the windows. (e.g., GNOME, KDE Plasma).
  • Wayland: The modern protocol that draws pixels on your screen. It replaced the 40-year-old X11 protocol and supports multi-monitor HDR and different refresh rates natively.
  • PipeWire: The modern audio and video handling system. If your Bluetooth headphones switch seamlessly, thank PipeWire.
  • Flatpak: The modern way to install apps. It's a universal package that works on ANY distro and is sandboxed for security.
  • GRUB: The bootloader. The menu you see when turning on a dual-boot PC.
